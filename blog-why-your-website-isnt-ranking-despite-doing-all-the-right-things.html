Why your website isn't ranking (despite doing all the right things)

You’ve installed Yoast, your WordPress SEO plugins.

You’ve added meta descriptions.

You’ve published a few blog posts (or had ChatGPT draft them for you).

You’ve even Googled your own site a few times just to check.

And yet… nothing. No clicks. No visibility.

Maybe a few impressions, but no one’s actually visiting.

You’re doing what SEO advice tells you to do — so why isn’t it working?

SEO isn’t broken — your content setup might be

Most SEO advice online is either vague (“just create quality content!”) or hyper‑specific in a way that’s unhelpful (“your H2 should have a long‑tail keyword about your niche service sector”).

And for expert‑led teams — consultants, creatives, scientists, founders — that advice usually misses the point. You’re not trying to spam keywords. You’re trying to be found for what you actually do.

Here’s where things typically go wrong:

1. You’re ranking for the wrong things

You might be showing up in search, but for low‑intent keywords that don’t convert. For example: a blog titled “Why We Love Research” won’t help if people are searching “best CRO for biotech partnerships”.

2. You’ve got titles and metadata that don’t invite clicks

If your meta descriptions are just a vague summary of the blog, or your page titles are too clever for their own good, people won’t click — even if you do rank. (One of my own pages got over 6,000 impressions and 0 clicks. Zero.)

3. You’re writing in isolation — not building a system

You write one blog post. Then another. And another. But they don’t link to each other. They don’t support your services page. They don’t rank collectively. They’re just… floating.

4. Your website and content don’t speak the same language

Your homepage says one thing. Your blog says another. Your About page is outdated. Google can’t tell what you’re trying to rank for — and neither can your audience.

So what actually creates website traffic?

Not more content. Not more tools.

What works is a system that supports your content from the start — one that’s:

  • Built on real search terms your audience actually uses
  • Structured so your website, blog, and services pages reinforce each other
  • Designed to scale — so you can repurpose and reuse content without rewriting everything
  • Clear, so people want to click through when they see your page in search
